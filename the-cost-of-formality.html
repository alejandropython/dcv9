Economic Analysis January 7, 2026

The Cost of Formality

"Slowly at first; and then all at once" — Ernest Hemingway

Back to Insights

There are two ways a country loses its competitive position.

The first is dramatic: currency collapse, expropriation, political rupture.

The second is gradual: institutional decay so normalized that it becomes invisible to those inside it.

Mexico is experiencing the second.

The Cost No Model Captures

Every underwriting model accounts for taxes, labor costs, currency risk. Few account for friction—the accumulated weight of systems designed not to function, but to persist.

Consider what the World Bank found before it discontinued its Doing Business report: Mexico ranked 120th globally in paying taxes. Not only because of rates, but because of what happens after you file.

The Post-Filing Index—a measure of what it takes to correct an error, claim a refund, or resolve a dispute with the tax authority—scored Mexico at 40.5 out of 100. The OECD average is 86.7. Brazil, infamous for bureaucratic complexity, scored 7.8.

Mexico is closer to Brazil than to its trade partners.

Request a VAT refund? In the United States: eight weeks. In Mexico: forty-two.

The numbers translate into time.

Discover an error in your corporate tax filing and attempt to correct it? The World Bank measured the process at 87 weeks. Nearly two years for a correction you initiated.

Our recent interaction with Mexico's tax authority was true to form. That is precisely the problem. Appointments that don't exist, criteria that shift between officials, a bureaucracy that neither approves nor denies, but simply delays. The implicit message to any enterprise seeking formality: stay small, stay quiet, stay informal.

The Architecture of Friction

What makes Mexico's institutional decay distinctive is not its severity but its comprehensiveness. The friction is not concentrated in one agency or one process. It is distributed across the entire architecture of doing business.

Starting a company costs 16.3% of income per capita—five times the OECD average.

Construction permits run 10.9% of warehouse value versus 1.5% elsewhere.

Want to connect a facility to the electrical grid? Budget 264% of per capita income for the privilege. In OECD countries, the same connection costs 63%.

That's a 4.2x multiplier before you've generated a single kilowatt of revenue. The pattern reveals itself not in any single data point but in their accumulation.

Each friction, individually, seems manageable. Together, they constitute a tax on formality that compounds with every transaction.

The Corruption That Doesn't Make Headlines

Large-scale corruption attracts attention. Small-scale corruption attracts resignation.

The file that disappears. The signature that never arrives. The process that restarts without explanation.

All of this. Wasting time.

Mexican businesses spend 241 hours per year on tax compliance alone, 50% more than their American counterparts.

Though still better than the Latin American average of 317 hours, the issue is not that Mexico is worst in class—the issue is that we are competing against OECD economies while operating with outdated infrastructure.

This inefficiency is structural, embedded in systems that were never designed to communicate.

The Nearshoring Paradox

The thesis writes itself: global supply chains are restructuring, proximity to the U.S. matters more than ever, and Mexico sits at the geographic center of the opportunity.

And yet.

The same week a multinational announces a Monterrey expansion, a mid-sized Mexican company quietly opens a Delaware LLC. The same month foreign direct investment hits a record, a generation of young professionals update their LinkedIn locations to Austin, Miami, Madrid.

Institutions do not fail all at once. They fail when the cost of compliance exceeds the cost of evasion. When formality becomes punishment rather than protection. When the honest operator looks at the informal one and begins to question his own choices. Mexico has not crossed that threshold. But the distance is narrowing.

At Denaris, we believe in the long-term potential of the region. But we also believe in seeing clearly. And what we see is a country where the distance between opportunity and execution remains, year after year, frustratingly wide.

Capital will continue to flow. The only question is whether Mexico will be a destination or a detour.
